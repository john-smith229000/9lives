# Character Bible (Day 1 depth)

Granular "who are these people, really" pass — voice, wants, wounds, subtext, and the small
physical details that make them read as alive. **Roles only, no names** (the friend cat
included). Setting assumption: a small, fog-bound harbor town of animals, cozy but wistful;
personalities are species-agnostic, so swap freely.

## The town's shared wound (the air everyone breathes)

Since the beacon went dark, **nothing comes in or out by sea**, and the town has turned
inward. Evenings "close over." People keep their trades out of ritual more than trade — the
fish seller with nothing new to sell, the cook feeding a thinning room. Nobody names it, but
the town is **grieving something it can't point to** — the same shape as the cat's frozen
day. Day 1's job is to make the player *feel* that held breath before they understand it.
Everyone is kind. Nobody can help. That gap is the emotional engine.

## The two cats

### You — the orange cat (silent protagonist)
- **Essence:** the one who *stays*. Observant, tender, far more frightened than the surface
  shows. You don't narrate fear; you narrate the world, and the fear leaks through in what
  you notice (their breath, the empty chairs, the dark lighthouse).
- **Voice:** you don't speak. The narration is your interiority — close, plain, present.
  Rule: **it never says "afraid," it shows you counting breaths.**
- **Want / need:** find help for the friend / to not be powerless. The loop is your terror
  made literal.
- **Arc:** tenderness → the horror of the loop → stubborn persistence → learning to hope.
- **Detail:** you keep glancing back toward home all day.

### The friend — the grey-and-white cat, sick (unnamed)
- **Essence:** the one who used to look after *you*. Dry, teasing, quietly formidable, and
  now dependent — which they hate gently. More frightened *for you* than for themselves.
- **Voice:** soft, wry, deflecting; starts sentences with energy and **runs out of breath
  halfway** (the ellipsis is their punctuation); turns concern back on you.
- **Want / need:** for you to go live the day, not hover / to still be a person to you, not
  a patient.
- **Arc (imperfect loop):** slips a little each loop — eats the fish, then picks at it, then
  leaves it ("you have it"); the ear that stayed up when they faked sleep starts staying
  down. On the last night, a frightened honesty.
- **Detail:** tucks their nose under one paw when pretending to sleep so you'll go — you can
  always tell, because the ear stays up (until, late, it doesn't).

## The townsfolk (by role)

### The marina / wharf worker
- A **young man, easygoing to the point of lazy** — sooner naps on a coil of rope than fixes
  his slip. Not unkind, just unbothered; needs a nudge to do anything.
- **Voice:** loose, breezy, a shrug in words — "yeah, I'll get to it" (he won't).
- **Wound / why he's stuck:** apathy more than despair — with the harbour shut there's "no
  point" hurrying, so he's let things slide (the jammed slip is his, unfixed for weeks).
- **Holds:** the crates/barrels; the jammed slip. **Arc:** the cat's stubbornness slowly
  shames — then inspires — him into pitching in.
- **Detail:** a favourite sun-napping spot, a half-whittled bit of wood, jobs left half-done.

### The larger-ship dock worker (dock master)
- An **older man wound tight with stress** — the harbour is his responsibility and it's
  falling apart on his watch. He buries the panic in procedure; the register is his life-raft.
- **Voice:** harried, clipped, fraying into snappishness; softens a beat when he forgets to be
  stressed, then catches himself.
- **Wound:** waiting for ships that don't come, accountable for a harbour he can't fix.
- **Subtext / seed:** the register **lists a vessel as due** — the strongest ship-seed,
  delivered as anxious bureaucracy, his pen hovering over a line he can't fill in.
- **Holds:** the fouled berth. **Detail:** ink-stained fingers, a stopped clock he keeps
  checking, the big stamped ledger, the unfilled line.

### The fish-market seller
- Brusque, weather-wise, warm under the crust. Reads sky and tide like scripture.
- **Voice:** clipped trade-talk; affection disguised as gruffness; ends soft moments by
  shoving food at you.
- **Wound:** with the harbor shut, nothing new comes in; her livelihood is dying and pride
  won't let her say it, so she talks tides.
- **Subtext / seed:** she's **counting the days** since a ship came, exactly — later she's
  the one who knows the schedule and the vet aboard.
- **Holds:** fish; a rat problem. **Detail:** cold red hands, a knife always moving, wraps
  fish in yesterday's paper without looking.

### The café owner  (café / bookshop; the clue-hub)
- A **warm, sweet lady who adores cats** — dotes on you and slips you **treats and little
  things to take home to your friend**. She also keeps the town's books, so she's the
  **clue-hub**: gently observant, she feels the town's held breath more than anyone.
- **Voice:** kind, a little dreamy, fusses over you — then says something uncannily
  perceptive and lets it hang.
- **Wound:** loneliness; two cups poured from habit, one always goes cold.
- **Subtext / seed:** senses **time itself is off** ("the evenings just close over") —
  closest to the loop, never quite names it.
- **Holds / gives:** treats + small gifts for the friend (comfort items; feeds the friend's
  "something to look at" ritual and G3 care); lighthouse lore, the clue thread.
- **Detail:** a saucer set down for you, dust in grey light, a barometer stuck on CHANGE,
  a door bell that hasn't rung in days.

### The general-store / grocer
- A **gruff, sour man who dislikes cats** — sure you're only here to **steal his food**. He
  shoos you off, watches you sidelong. Tight-fisted, rationing what little's left.
- **Voice:** curt, suspicious, grumbling — "shoo," "paws off my shelves."
- **Wound:** can't restock with the harbour shut; guards his dwindling stock like treasure
  and takes it out on the nearest stray.
- **Arc:** when you **catch the rat that's been raiding his store** (a later day), he
  softens — grudging thanks turns to real warmth; you become the one cat he trusts.
- **Holds:** lamp oil, a rat trap — and a **storm-stranded delivery**. **Detail:** half-empty
  shelves guarded like a vault, an apron of patched pockets, a broom he waves at cats.

### The retiree(s)  (a sweet old couple)
- A **sweet old married couple** who **bicker fondly** over every little thing — the town's
  memory and its comic relief. They remember when the light was lit and the quay ran gold.
- **Voice:** overlapping, correcting each other's years and stories; softens into real grief
  for the old town, then straight back to bickering.
- **Wound / seed:** they've outlived the town's good years and half-believe they won't return.
- **Holds:** small nuisances (a rat in the shed; a thing stuck just out of reach); shortcuts
  and lore.

### The mother  *(a separate townsperson — the town's "kept hoping" pole)*
- Not a caretaker of the sick — **ordinary life continuing**, and the one who **never stopped
  hoping**. Her grown child (or partner) **sailed out on the lifeline ship before the harbour
  shut** and can't get back; she keeps a **lamp burning in her window** for them, every night,
  the way she always has.
- **Thematic role:** she completes a triangle of responses to the same fear of loss — the
  **keeper abandoned hope** (let the great light die); **you froze in fear** (the loop); the
  **mother kept hope alive** (her small window lamp). You spend the game moving from the
  keeper's pole toward hers. Her lamp is the quiet argument that hope was right all along.
- **Voice:** tired warmth stretched thin; a brave, everyday face; matter-of-fact about the
  lamp, as if it's nothing.
- **Function:** a warm counterweight to the keeper's darkness; a small **domestic quest**
  (the pantry rat, Day 4); and a **Day 9 payoff** — the one she waited for steps off the
  same ship that brings the vet (hope, rewarded, in parallel with the cure arriving).
- **Detail:** the window lamp; a place set at the table that stays empty — until it doesn't.

### The keeper's sick partner  *(the keeper's sole loved one)*
- Not a "character" you talk to — a presence behind a door: a cough, a still shape, a
  **model boat / a ship painting by the bed** (they were a sailor, once). The one the keeper
  cannot leave, and who the ship's **vet** would save. The keeper's whole world.
- **Function:** the reason the keeper is trapped; the mirror of your friend. What you do for
  your friend, you learn to do for the partner (fish, quiet, the bedside rat, sitting the
  vigil so the keeper can rest) — and *that* is what thaws the keeper and earns the key.

### The lighthouse keeper (glimpsed Day 1, met Day 2) — the mirror

The heart of the theme. Deep-dive:

- **Who they were:** the lightkeeper who kept the town tied to the world — every ship that
  ever found the harbour found it by their flame. A steady, proud heart of the place. Shame
  under the despair now, because they know exactly what the dark lighthouse means.
- **Why they stopped:** their **partner fell ill**, and there is **no one else** — the keeper
  is the sole caretaker, and cannot leave the bedside for the hours (and nights) it takes to
  restore and *tend* the beacon. And after enough dark nights with no ship answering, they
  decided the light was **pointless** — that hoping only buys you the fall. So they let the
  beacon die and kept only the **small bedside lamp**. (Practical *and* emotional — both true.)
- **Why the player can when they can't:** the beacon needs the lens recovered, oil, the stair
  repaired, and an all-night vigil — days of legwork and hours away from a bedside. The
  player is **free** to do it; the keeper is not. That's the crux of the whole game.
- **The two lights (their signature image):** they can tend a **bedside lamp** but not the
  **beacon** — private care they can still bear, public hope they can't. One lit window
  under all that dark. Relighting the big light = daring to hope again.
- **Voice (Day 2+):** flat, tired, gently pessimistic; never cruel, just emptied. Doesn't
  argue — just doesn't believe. Every soft refusal is aimed at the version of themselves
  that used to hope.
- **Wound:** hoping means risking loss, and they can't survive being let down again — the
  exact bargain you're making with the loop. Your refusal to give up is unbearable to be
  near, because it mirrors who they were.
- **Subtext (never stated):** the ship's vet would save their partner too. Easing their
  burden and lighting the beacon are the **same act** — the quiet thesis.
- **Arc:** despair → grudging trust as you quietly help care for the partner and the town →
  letting themselves hope, the same hour you do (Day 8). On the last climb you don't convince
  them; you refuse to leave until they come up the stairs beside you.
- **Detail:** the single lit window; a chair worn smooth beside a bed you glimpse but never
  enter; a keeper's key they've stopped wearing; a logbook of ships that stops mid-page.

## Motifs planted Day 1 (paid off later)
- *"We look after our own."* → what you extend to the keeper.
- *"Help comes from off the island."* → the ship / the vet.
- *"The evenings just close over."* → the loop itself.
- *"Something's better than nothing."* → your refusal to give up.
- *Small kindnesses hold the place together.* → the beacon is a chain of small mends.

## Decisions
- **Locked (this pass):** the keeper is the **sole** caretaker of a **sick partner** — a
  clean mirror of you (alone with a sick companion): two cottages, two lone caretakers, two
  small lights. The mother is a **separate** townsperson (waiting on the sea), not part of
  the lighthouse. See `continuity_audit.md`.
- Still open: keep the mother or cut her; the keeper's partner (spouse / companion / sibling)
  and whether they were a sailor (the boat plant); setting species; the two cats' history;
  names.
